    \section{Problem Definition}

    Housing-market risk depends on multiple economic and structural factors, but
    these indicators are often analyzed separately, limiting holistic understanding
    and cross-region comparison.

    The RHFI project addresses this by creating a unified, data-driven framework
    and interactive visualization system to measure and analyze housing fragility.

    Key challenges include:
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
        \item Lack of integrated risk metrics
        \item Limited transparency of indicator contributions
        \item Poor comparability across regions
        \item Limited interactivity in existing tools
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Literature Survey}

    Research on housing-market fragility highlights the importance of combining
    multiple indicators—such as price-to-income ratios, rent burden, employment,
    and inventory—to assess regional vulnerability. The following studies form the
    foundation of the RHFI framework.

    \subsection{Glaeser \& Gyourko (2018)}
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
        \item Show that supply constraints increase price volatility and fragility.
        \item Support use of structural indicators like price-to-income and inventory.
        \item Limitation: Focuses mainly on supply-side factors.
    \end{itemize}

    \subsection{Gabriel \& Rosenthal (2015)}
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
        \item Emphasize that affordability is multidimensional (income, rent, demographics).
        \item Justify RHFI’s composite index approach.
        \item Limitation: Focuses on affordability, not overall fragility.
    \end{itemize}

    \subsection{Anenberg \& Ringo (2022)}
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
        \item Link market tightness (prices, inventory, vacancies) to vulnerability.
        \item Support use of growth indicators as early warning signals.
        \item Limitation: Focuses on short-term dynamics over long-term fragility.
    \end{itemize}
    \section{System Architecture}
    The system follows a modular architecture consisting of three major layers:

    \subsection{Data Layer}
    The data layer consumes the unified dataset produced in Step 1 of the project. It includes:

    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
        \item Zillow Home Value Index (ZHVI)
        \item Income, rent, and employment datasets
        \item Derived indicators such as price-to-income ratio, rent growth, and job growth
    \end{itemize}

    All data is preprocessed and stored in a master dataset (\texttt{final\_dataset.csv}), which is loaded by the visualization system at runtime.

    \subsubsection{Normalized Home Price Values}

    A key preprocessing step in the unified dataset is the normalization of the
    \texttt{Home\_price} variable. Because raw Zillow home values vary widely across
    regions, min--max scaling is applied to map all values to the interval $[0,1]$.
    This transformation sets the lowest observed home price to 0 and the highest to
    1. For instance, the 2015 home price for Region~10001 normalizes to roughly
    0.32, meaning it is about 32\% of the maximum value in the dataset. This
    standardization ensures comparability across heterogeneous markets and prevents
    large-magnitude variables from dominating the RHFI computation.


    \[
        x_{\text{scaled}} = \frac{x - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}
    \]

    \subsection{Innovations}

    Our approach introduces several innovations across both the computational and visualization layers:

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Composite Fragility Index (RHFI):}
        A unified metric integrating six normalized indicators---home price, rent, PIR, price growth, rent growth, and inventory growth---into a single interpretable score of regional housing fragility.

        \item \textbf{Interactive Multi-Layer Visualization System:}
        A Streamlit-based dashboard combining geographic heatmaps, temporal trend explorers, region comparison tools, and indicator-level breakdowns to support exploratory analysis.

        \item \textbf{Dynamic Indicator Contribution Analysis:}
        A transparent decomposition of RHFI into indicator-level contributions, enabling users to understand the drivers of fragility for any region and year.

        \item \textbf{Forecasting Module Using Prophet:}
        A forward-looking analytical layer that projects future housing indicators with configurable parameters and uncertainty bands, supporting scenario exploration.
    \end{enumerate}

    These innovations collectively enhance interpretability, interactivity, and analytical depth beyond traditional housing-market dashboards.


    \subsection{UI Layer}

        \item A geographic heatmap of RHFI scores
        \item A time slider for exploring trends from 2005--2024
        \item A region comparison module
        \item Indicator breakdown visualizations
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Experiments}

    We evaluate the RHFI framework and visualization system through experiments assessing robustness, interpretability, and predictive value.

    \subsection{RHFI Stability and Sensitivity}
    Analyze RHFI evolution over time and response to $\pm 10\%$ changes in indicator weights.
    \textbf{Metrics:} year-to-year variance, sensitivity curves, correlation with indicators.

    \subsection{Validation Against Historical Events}
    Compare RHFI trends with macroeconomic shocks (e.g., 2008 recession, 2020 pandemic).
    \textbf{Metrics:} lead-lag correlation with home price drops, RHFI spike detection, regional clustering.

    \subsection{Forecasting Evaluation}
    Assess accuracy of Prophet predictions for home prices, rent, and employment.
    \textbf{Metrics:} RMSE, MAPE, confidence interval coverage.

    \subsection{Usability and Visualization}
    Conduct informal user testing for dashboard clarity and interpretability.
    \textbf{Metrics:} task completion time, clarity scores, interpretation errors.

    These experiments validate both the computational and visualization components of RHFI.

    \section{Overview}
    The Real Estate Housing Fragility Index (RHFI) project quantifies and visualizes
    regional housing‑market vulnerability across the United States. As markets grow
    more dynamic and sensitive to economic shifts, policymakers require tools that
    reveal emerging risks, structural imbalances, and long‑term trends. RHFI
    integrates multiple indicators—including home prices, income, rent, employment,
    and derived ratios—into a unified measure of housing fragility.

    A central component of the project is the visualization and system design
    framework, which transforms raw datasets and computed RHFI scores into an
    interactive Streamlit dashboard. This interface supports both high‑level pattern
    recognition and detailed indicator‑level analysis.

    The visualization platform is designed to:
    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep,leftmargin=*]
        \item Present RHFI scores geographically and temporally.
        \item Enable cross‑region comparison through interactive charts.
        \item Provide transparency via indicator‑level contributions.
        \item Support exploratory analysis for analysts and policymakers.
    \end{itemize}

    Together, these components form a comprehensive analytical tool that makes
    housing fragility patterns accessible, interpretable, and actionable.

    \section{Problem Definition}

    Housing-market risk is influenced by a complex interplay of economic, demographic, and structural factors. Traditional reporting tools often present these indicators in isolation, making it difficult for analysts to identify patterns or assess fragility holistically. Furthermore, regional disparities in home prices, income levels, and rental markets create challenges for cross-region comparison.

    The RHFI project addresses these challenges by defining a unified, data-driven framework for measuring housing fragility and presenting it through an interactive visualization system. The core problem can be summarized as follows:

    \begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
        \item \textbf{Lack of integrated risk metrics:} Existing tools do not combine multiple housing indicators into a single interpretable index.
        \item \textbf{Limited transparency:} Users cannot easily understand how individual indicators contribute to overall risk.
        \item \textbf{Poor comparability:} Differences in regional scale and magnitude make direct comparison difficult without normalization and standardization.
        \item \textbf{Insufficient interactivity:} Static charts and reports do not support exploratory analysis or dynamic filtering.
    \end{itemize}

    The RHFI visualization system is designed to solve these issues by providing a unified, interactive platform for analyzing regional housing fragility.

    \section{Literature Survey}

    Research on housing-market fragility draws from real estate economics, financial stability analysis, and urban policy. Prior studies emphasize the need to integrate multiple indicators—such as price-to-income ratios, rent burden, employment trends, and inventory dynamics—to assess regional vulnerability. The following works provide the conceptual foundation for the RHFI framework.

    \subsection{Glaeser \& Gyourko (2018): Housing Supply and Market Volatility}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Analyze how zoning and regulatory constraints amplify price volatility and long-term fragility in metropolitan housing markets.
        \item Demonstrate that supply-inelastic regions experience sharper boom–bust cycles and heightened affordability stress.
        \item Motivate RHFI’s inclusion of structural indicators such as price-to-income ratio and inventory growth.
        \item Limitation: Focuses primarily on supply elasticity and gives limited attention to demand-side shocks (e.g., employment, migration).
    \end{itemize}

    \subsection{Gabriel \& Rosenthal (2015): Multidimensional Housing Affordability}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Examine affordability stress across U.S. metropolitan areas using rent burden, income trends, and demographic factors.
        \item Argue that affordability is multidimensional and cannot be captured by a single metric.
        \item Support RHFI’s composite-index approach and the need for normalization across regions.
        \item Limitation: Centers on affordability rather than systemic fragility, leaving broader risk integration to future work.
    \end{itemize}

    \subsection{Anenberg \& Ringo (2022): Market Tightness and Vulnerability}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Propose a framework for measuring housing-market tightness using vacancy rates, price acceleration, and inventory dynamics.
        \item Show that tight markets are more vulnerable to macroeconomic shocks and exhibit greater instability.
        \item Align with RHFI’s inclusion of price growth, rent growth, and inventory change as early-warning indicators.
        \item Limitation: Emphasizes short-term tightness rather than long-term structural fragility, which RHFI aims to capture.
    \end{itemize}

    \section{Experiments}

    To evaluate the RHFI framework and visualization system, we will conduct a series of experiments designed to assess robustness, interpretability, and predictive value.

    \subsection{RHFI Stability and Sensitivity Analysis}
    We will analyze how RHFI scores evolve over time and how sensitive they are to changes in indicator weights.
    \textbf{Metrics:}
    year-to-year RHFI variance, sensitivity curves under $\pm 10\%$ weight perturbations, and correlation between RHFI and underlying indicators.

    \subsection{Validation Against Historical Economic Events}
    We will compare RHFI trends with known macroeconomic shocks (e.g., 2008 recession, 2020 pandemic).
    \textbf{Metrics:}
    lead-lag correlation with home price declines, RHFI spike detection accuracy, and regional clustering during downturns.

    \subsection{Forecasting Evaluation}
    Using Prophet-generated predictions, we will evaluate forecasting accuracy for home prices, rent, and employment.
    \textbf{Metrics:}
    RMSE, MAPE, and confidence interval coverage.

    \subsection{Usability and Visualization Effectiveness}
    We will conduct informal user testing to evaluate dashboard clarity and interpretability.
    \textbf{Metrics:}
    task completion time, user-reported clarity scores, and error rates in interpreting indicator contributions.

    These experiments will validate both the computational and visualization components of the RHFI system.
